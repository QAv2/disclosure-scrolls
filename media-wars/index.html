The Science

The Architects of Consent

Before there was media control, there was media science. In the 1920s, two men — one the nephew of Sigmund Freud, the other a presidential advisor — designed the intellectual framework for controlling what entire populations believe. They didn’t hide it. They published it.

Documented
Edward Bernays, nephew of Sigmund Freud, published Propaganda in 1928. Opening line: “The conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is an important element in democratic society. Those who manipulate this unseen mechanism of society constitute an invisible government which is the true ruling power of our country.” Bernays coined the term “public relations” as a euphemism for propaganda.
Edward Bernays, Propaganda (1928, Horace Liveright). Archive.org: full text. Larry Tye, The Father of Spin (1998).
Documented
Walter Lippmann, advisor to President Wilson and co-founder of The New Republic, coined the phrase “manufacture of consent” in his 1922 book Public Opinion. He argued that the general public is incapable of understanding complex affairs and that a “specialized class” must manage public perception on their behalf. The phrase was later adopted by Noam Chomsky as the title of his landmark media criticism.
Walter Lippmann, Public Opinion (1922). Archive.org: full text. Noam Chomsky & Edward Herman, Manufacturing Consent (1988).

The Consolidation

Operation Mockingbird required placing individual agents in individual newsrooms. That was expensive and fragile. The consolidation wave of the 1990s solved both problems: instead of infiltrating 50 companies, you only need to control 6.

50 → 6

Documented
In 1983, approximately 50 companies controlled the majority of American media. By 2024, that number had consolidated to 6: Comcast (NBCUniversal), Disney (ABC, ESPN, Hulu), News Corp/Fox Corporation (Fox News, Wall Street Journal), Warner Bros. Discovery (CNN, HBO), Paramount Global (CBS, MTV, Nickelodeon), and Sony (Columbia Pictures). These corporations control approximately 90% of what Americans read, watch, and listen to.
Ben Bagdikian, The Media Monopoly (1983, updated editions through 2004). FCC ownership data. Columbia Journalism Review media consolidation tracker.
Documented
The Telecommunications Act of 1996 — the first major overhaul of US telecommunications law in 62 years — eliminated most ownership caps on radio stations and relaxed TV ownership limits. Within a year, radio station ownership consolidated dramatically: Clear Channel (now iHeartMedia) grew from 40 stations to over 1,200.
Telecommunications Act of 1996 (Public Law 104-104). FCC ownership data. Columbia Journalism Review.

Act IV

The Legalization

For 65 years, it was illegal for the US government to direct propaganda at its own citizens. In 2013, buried inside a must-pass defense bill, that protection was quietly removed.

Smith-Mundt

Documented
The Smith-Mundt Act (1948) explicitly prohibited domestic dissemination of materials produced by the State Department and US government broadcasting entities — Voice of America, Radio Free Europe — preventing government-produced propaganda directed abroad from being turned inward against American citizens.
Public Law 80-402 (1948). Congressional Record.
Documented
The Smith-Mundt Modernization Act — Section 1078 of the NDAA FY2013, signed by President Obama on January 2, 2013 — struck the domestic dissemination ban. State Department and US Agency for Global Media materials with an annual budget of ~$810 million, broadcasting in 60+ languages, can now legally target American citizens. It was embedded in a must-pass defense authorization bill and passed with minimal public debate.
Public Law 112-239, Section 1078. Foreign Policy Magazine (2013). Congress.gov (H.R. 5736).

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
Extract files hidden in a CNTYSEED steganographic image.
Only requires: pip install Pillow
Usage: python3 extract.py <image.png> <output_dir>
"""
import hashlib, io, os, struct, sys, tarfile, zlib
from PIL import Image

MAGIC = b"CNTYSEED"
HDR_SIZE = 64
HDR_FMT = "!8sBBBBII32sHH8s"


def lsb_decode(flat, depth, num_bytes):
    total_bits = num_bytes * 8
    pad = depth - (total_bits % depth) if total_bits % depth else 0
    slots = (total_bits + pad) // depth
    mask = (1 << depth) - 1
    bits = []
    for i in range(slots):
        val = flat[i] & mask
        for shift in range(depth - 1, -1, -1):
            bits.append((val >> shift) & 1)
    out = bytearray()
    for i in range(0, num_bytes * 8, 8):
        byte = 0
        for b in range(8):
            byte = (byte << 1) | bits[i + b]
        out.append(byte)
    return bytes(out)


def extract(image_path, output_dir):
    img = Image.open(image_path).convert("RGB")
    flat = [ch for px in img.getdata() for ch in px]

    hdr = None
    for depth in (1, 2, 3, 4):
        raw = lsb_decode(flat, depth, HDR_SIZE)
        if raw[:8] != MAGIC:
            continue
        fields = struct.unpack(HDR_FMT, raw)
        if fields[3] == depth:
            hdr = {
                "flags": fields[2], "lsb_depth": depth,
                "compression": fields[4], "original_size": fields[5],
                "compressed_size": fields[6], "sha256": fields[7],
            }
            break

    if not hdr:
        print("No embedded data found.")
        sys.exit(1)

    if hdr["flags"] & 0x01:
        print("This image is encrypted.")
        sys.exit(1)

    print(f"Found: {hdr['original_size']:,} bytes, {hdr['lsb_depth']}-LSB")

    total = HDR_SIZE + hdr["compressed_size"]
    raw = lsb_decode(flat, hdr["lsb_depth"], total)
    payload = raw[HDR_SIZE:]

    if hdr["compression"] == 1:
        payload = zlib.decompress(payload)
    elif hdr["compression"] == 2:
        import lzma
        payload = lzma.decompress(payload)

    if hashlib.sha256(payload).digest() == hdr["sha256"]:
        print("Integrity: VERIFIED")
    else:
        print("WARNING: SHA-256 mismatch")

    os.makedirs(output_dir, exist_ok=True)
    if hdr["flags"] & 0x02:
        with tarfile.open(fileobj=io.BytesIO(payload), mode="r") as tar:
            tar.extractall(path=output_dir, filter="data")
        print(f"Extracted to: {output_dir}/")
    else:
        with open(os.path.join(output_dir, "extracted"), "wb") as f:
            f.write(payload)
        print(f"Written to: {output_dir}/extracted")


if __name__ == "__main__":
    if len(sys.argv) != 3:
        print(f"Usage: {sys.argv[0]} <image.png> <output_dir>")
        sys.exit(1)
    extract(sys.argv[1], sys.argv[2])
