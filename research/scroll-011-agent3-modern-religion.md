## Thread 4: Tax Exemption as Control Mechanism

### Key Facts

**The Structural Framework**
- Under US tax law (Internal Revenue Code Section 501(c)(3)), churches and religious organizations are automatically tax-exempt. Unlike all other 501(c)(3) organizations, churches are not required to apply for tax-exempt status — they receive it automatically. **[DOCUMENTED — GREEN]**
- Churches are also exempt from filing IRS Form 990 (the annual information return required of other nonprofits). This means there is no public disclosure of their finances — no revenue figures, no salary data, no expenditure breakdowns. This is unique among tax-exempt organizations. **[DOCUMENTED — GREEN]**
- Churches are exempt from property taxes on their buildings and land. In many cities, church-owned real estate represents a significant portion of the tax base that is not contributing to public services. **[DOCUMENTED — GREEN]**

**The Dollar Amount**
- The estimated total value of religious tax exemptions in the US: approximately $71 billion annually, according to a widely cited analysis by University of Tampa professor Ryan Cragun and colleagues, published in *Free Inquiry* magazine (2012). - The parsonage allowance (IRC Section 107) allows ministers to deduct housing costs from taxable income. This benefit is estimated at $800 million annually. The Freedom From Religion Foundation challenged this provision; a federal appeals court upheld it in 2019. **[DOCUMENTED — GREEN]**

**The Johnson Amendment (1954)**
- Senator Lyndon B. Johnson introduced an amendment to the tax code in 1954 that prohibits all 501(c)(3) organizations, including churches, from endorsing or opposing political candidates. The amendment was not specifically targeted at churches — Johnson was reportedly motivated by secular nonprofit groups opposing his Senate re-election. **[DOCUMENTED — GREEN]**
- The Johnson Amendment has been rarely enforced against churches. The IRS effectively ceased auditing churches after a series of legal and political challenges.
