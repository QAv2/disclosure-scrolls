## ACT I — THE MONOPOLY

### 1. Standard Oil: 91% refining, 85% sales by 1904
**VERIFIED.** Multiple sources confirm 91% of US oil refining and 85% of final sales by 1904. Note: by the time of the 1911 ruling, market share had fallen to ~64%. The scroll should acknowledge this decline existed — it strengthens the argument that the breakup was performative, since the monopoly was already eroding naturally.

### 2. Supreme Court dissolution 1911 into 34 companies
**VERIFIED with caveat.** The Supreme Court ordered dissolution under the Sherman Antitrust Act in *Standard Oil Co. of New Jersey v. United States*, 221 U.S. 1 (1911). Most sources say 34 companies; some say 33 or 38-39 depending on counting method. **Use "34" — it's the most commonly cited figure** and matches the court record.

### 3. Reconsolidation mergers
**MOSTLY VERIFIED — ONE CORRECTION NEEDED.**
- ExxonMobil (1999): Exxon (Standard Oil NJ) + Mobil (Standard Oil NY) = CORRECT
- ChevronTexaco (2001): Standard Oil CA merged with Gulf Oil (1984) and then Texaco (2001) = CORRECT (later reverted to just "Chevron")
- **ConocoPhillips (2002): CORRECTION NEEDED.** Continental Oil (Conoco) was indeed a Standard Oil subsidiary, but the plan says "Standard Oil IN + Continental." Standard Oil of Indiana became **Amoco**, which was acquired by **BP** in 1998 — not ConocoPhillips. ConocoPhillips = Conoco (Continental Oil, a minor Standard Oil fragment) + Phillips Petroleum (NOT a Standard Oil company). **Fix the grid: replace "Standard Oil IN + Continental Oil → ConocoPhillips" with the correct lineage, or drop ConocoPhillips from the "reconsolidation" narrative since Phillips was never Standard Oil.**
- BP (1998-2000): BP acquired Amoco (Standard Oil IN) in 1998, then ARCO (Atlantic Richfield, which had absorbed Standard Oil OH/Sohio) in 2000 = CORRECT

### 4. Rockefeller family retained stock across all successors
**VERIFIED.** JD Rockefeller held >25% of Standard Oil at breakup. All shareholders received proportional shares in all 34 successors. His wealth rose from ~$300M to $900M in the decade after dissolution — the breakup made him richer. The "dissolution redistributed operational control without eliminating ownership" framing is accurate.

### 5. Ida Tarbell's book — date and role
**VERIFIED.** Serialized in McClure's Magazine November 1902 through October 1904. Published as a book in 1904 by McClure, Phillips & Co. The plan correctly cites 1904. The work is widely credited with accelerating the antitrust case that led to the 1911 ruling.

## ACT II — THE CARTEL

### 1. Achnacarry Agreement (1928)
**VERIFIED with detail correction.** The meeting was at Achnacarry Castle, Scotland, in August 1928. Participants: Henri Deterding (Shell), Walter Teagle (Standard Oil NJ), and Sir John Cadman (Anglo-Persian/BP). William Mellon (Gulf Oil) also attended. The agreement was finalized September 17, 1928 and kept secret until 1952. **The plan says "Shell, Standard Oil NJ, Anglo-Persian" which is correct for the three signatories. Deterding organized it.**

### 2. FTC discovery and documentation
**VERIFIED.** The FTC produced "The International Petroleum Cartel" staff report, submitted to the Senate Subcommittee on Monopoly in **1952**. The report documented how seven companies controlled most of the world's petroleum through jointly owned subsidiaries. President Truman ordered a DOJ grand jury investigation (August 1952), but it was downgraded from criminal to civil before he left office. The civil case dragged on until 1968 without trial. **The plan says "decades later" — be precise: the FTC report came 24 years after Achnacarry.**

### 3. Seven Sisters controlled ~85% of global reserves
**VERIFIED.** By the 1950s-1960s, the Seven Sisters controlled approximately 85% of world petroleum reserves. This dominance held until the 1973 oil crisis and subsequent nationalizations. Today the old Seven Sisters (now four companies) control only ~3% of reserves and ~10% of production. **The percentage and timeframe (mid-20th century through early 1970s) are correct.**

### 4. Enrico Mattei coined the term
**CORRECTION NEEDED.** Mattei coined "sette sorelle" (Seven Sisters) in the **1950s**, likely after 1951 when ENI was denied entry to the Iranian oil consortium. The plan cites a "1968 speech" — **Mattei died in 1962, so a 1968 speech is impossible.** Anthony Sampson's 1975 book "The Seven Sisters" popularized the term in English. **Fix the source citation: remove the fabricated 1968 date. The term was coined in the 1950s; no single definitive speech date is documented.**

### 5. Mattei plane crash and sabotage ruling
**PARTIALLY VERIFIED — DATE CORRECTION.** Mattei died October 27, 1962 in a crash near Bascape, Lombardy — CORRECT. However, the sabotage finding came from the **1997** Pavia investigation (not 2005). The Procura di Pavia reopened the case in the mid-1990s using preserved wreckage fragments; forensic analysis identified explosive traces (TNT) and concluded the plane was destroyed by an onboard bomb triggered during landing gear deployment. **Change "2005" to "1997" in the evidence card.** The case remains partially unsolved (sabotage confirmed, perpetrators unidentified).

### 6. Henri Deterding — Shell founder and Achnacarry participant
**CORRECTION NEEDED.** Deterding was NOT the founder of Shell. Marcus Samuel founded the Shell Transport and Trading Company. Deterding joined Royal Dutch Oil Company in 1896 and became its CEO in 1901. He engineered the 1907 merger of Royal Dutch and Shell (Royal Dutch holding 60%). He was the driving force behind the Achnacarry meeting in 1928. By the 1920s, under his leadership, Royal Dutch Shell was the world's largest oil company. **Call him "Royal Dutch Shell chief" or "the man who built Shell into the world's largest oil company," not "founder."**
