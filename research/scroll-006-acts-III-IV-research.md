### CLAIM 1: Media Consolidation -- "50 companies in 1983 to 6 in 2024"

**CONFIRMED (with nuance needed)**

**The 50 figure (1983):** Ben Bagdikian's "The Media Monopoly" (1st edition, 1983, Beacon Press) concluded that "50 men and women, chiefs of their corporations, control more than half the information and ideas that reach 220 million Americans." The number 50 is correct for the 1983 first edition.

**Full Bagdikian progression across editions:**
- 1st edition (1983): 50 corporations
- 2nd edition (1987): 29 corporations (some sources say 26 -- this discrepancy appears across secondary sources; the difference may reflect different metrics used)
- 3rd edition (1990): 23 corporations
- 4th edition (1992): fewer than two dozen (~20)
- 5th edition (1997): 10 corporations
- 6th edition (2000): 6 corporations
- 7th edition (2004, retitled "The New Media Monopoly"): 5 corporations (AOL Time Warner, Bertelsmann, Disney, News Corporation, Viacom)

**The "6 in 2024" figure:** The "Big 6" shorthand is widely used but fluid. The original "Big 6" list (circa 2011 when the famous Frugal Dad infographic went viral) was: Comcast, Disney, News Corp, Time Warner, Viacom, and CBS Corporation. By 2024, mergers have reshuffled the deck: Viacom and CBS re-merged into Paramount Global, Time Warner became Warner Bros. Discovery, 21st Century Fox was partially acquired by Disney.

**CORRECTION: Sony's inclusion is debatable.** The build plan lists Sony as the 6th company. Sony is a major media conglomerate (Columbia Pictures, Sony Music, Crunchyroll) but was NOT part of the traditional "Big 6" that controlled 90% of US media. The traditional list was: Comcast/NBCUniversal, Disney, News Corp/Fox, Time Warner (now WBD), Viacom, and CBS (now both Paramount Global). Some 2024 lists substitute Amazon for older companies. The "6 corporations = 90%" stat originates from the ~2011 Frugal Dad infographic and refers to a specific historical configuration. As of 2024-2026, the landscape has shifted enough that the exact number depends on methodology.

**Recommendation:** The claim "50 companies in 1983, 6 in 2024" is defensible as a broad narrative, but the plan should either: (a) use Bagdikian's own endpoint of 5 in 2004 from the final edition, or (b) acknowledge the list is approximate and shifting. The "90%" figure is widely cited but its methodology is imprecise. Consider: "In 1983, Ben Bagdikian documented 50 corporations controlling the majority of American media. By his final edition in 2004, that number was 5."

**Sources:**
- Ben Bagdikian, "The Media Monopoly" (1983, Beacon Press) and subsequent editions
- FAIR, "Media Monopoly Revisited"
- Grand Rapids Institute for Information Democracy analysis
- Fortune, "These 6 Companies Control Much of U.S. Media"

### CLAIM 2: Telecommunications Act of 1996 -- "first major overhaul in 62 years"

**CONFIRMED**

The Telecommunications Act of 1996 (Public Law 104-104) was indeed the first major overhaul of US telecommunications law in nearly 62 years, amending the Communications Act of 1934. This exact language ("first major overhaul...in nearly 62 years") appears on the FCC's own website and in the Wikipedia article sourced from congressional records.

**Clear Channel 40 to 1,200 stations:**

**CORRECTION: The starting number needs adjustment.** The build plan says Clear Channel grew "from 40 stations to over 1,200." The 40 figure refers to the pre-1996 NATIONAL OWNERSHIP CAP (no single company could own more than 40 stations nationwide), not Clear Channel's actual holdings. Clear Channel owned approximately 36 radio stations and 10 TV stations at the end of 1995. Some sources do cite "40 stations" for Clear Channel specifically, suggesting the company was near the cap.

The 1,200+ endpoint is confirmed. Multiple sources cite Clear Channel growing to 1,200-1,240 stations after the Act. One FCC source says "over 1,600 stations" at its peak.

**Recommendation:** Use: "Clear Channel grew from roughly 40 stations to over 1,200" -- this is close enough and widely cited. Or more precisely: "Clear Channel grew from 36 stations to over 1,200 after the ownership cap was eliminated."

### CLAIM 4: CIA Entertainment Liaison

**CIA liaison founded 1996:** CONFIRMED. The CIA established a formal entertainment liaison office in 1996, within the Office of Public Affairs.

**Chase Brandon as first liaison:** CONFIRMED. Chase Brandon served as the CIA's first entertainment liaison officer from 1996 to early 2007.

**Tommy Lee Jones cousin:** CONFIRMED. Chase Brandon is the first cousin of Tommy Lee Jones. This is documented across multiple sources including Spy Culture's detailed profile and Tricia Jenkins's book.

**"60+ film and TV productions since 1947":** PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. The figure of 60 film and TV productions is cited in multiple secondary sources (Spyscape, The WAR Room, etc.) as the number the CIA has "assisted" since its founding in 1947. However, this appears to be a lowball estimate -- Secker and Alford's FOIA research suggests much more extensive involvement. The 60 figure likely represents only the publicly acknowledged collaborations. The build plan's phrasing "60+ film and TV productions" is supported by available sources.

**Mission: Impossible script changes:** CONFIRMED (with important nuance).

The build plan says the script was "changed to remove criticism of CIA black budgets and was altered to criticize the Church Committee instead." This is partially accurate but oversimplified:

- The original script contained a scene where a senator held forth on CIA secrecy, lack of accountability, and black budgets. This was diluted in the final version.
- In the finished film, the Church Committee reference appears as a NEGATIVE reference -- a TV host accuses the senator of wanting to "lead the sort of charge Frank Church led in the 1970s" and "destroy the intelligence capability of this country."
- The script was not "altered to criticize the Church Committee" -- rather, the Church Committee is referenced dismissively as a cautionary tale against oversight. The original, stronger criticism of CIA was softened.
- Chase Brandon is credited in the film and appears in a DVD special feature praising it.
- FOIA requests for CIA records on the film came up blank.

**CORRECTION:** The claim should be reworded. It was not simply "changed to remove criticism and criticize the Church Committee." More accurately: "The original script's criticism of CIA black budgets and accountability was diluted, and the Church Committee was referenced in a negative light -- framed as having damaged national security rather than provided necessary oversight."

**Animal Farm -- CIA or British IRD?**

**CORRECTION: It was the CIA, not primarily the British IRD.** The build plan says "The CIA secretly purchased the film rights to George Orwell's Animal Farm and altered the ending so the communists were overthrown." This is substantially correct:

- After Orwell's death in 1950, his widow Sonia Orwell sold the film rights to Carleton Alsop and Finis Farr -- undercover agents for the CIA's Office of Policy Coordination (the same OPC founded by Frank Wisner, the Mockingbird architect).
- The operation was connected to E. Howard Hunt's Psychological Warfare Workshop.
- Cold War historian Tony Shaw confirmed through archival research that the CIA altered the ending so the pigs (communists) were overthrown by the other animals.
- The British IRD (Information Research Department) was ALSO involved in Orwell-related propaganda operations -- distributing translations of Animal Farm and 1984 -- but the FILM RIGHTS PURCHASE was a CIA operation specifically.
- The 1954 animated film was directed by John Halas and Joy Batchelor.

The build plan is correct to attribute this to the CIA. The IRD had parallel operations but the film itself was a CIA project.

**Source for Animal Farm:** Tricia Jenkins, "The CIA in Hollywood" (2012); Tony Shaw, archival research; History.com; Spyscape detailed investigation; Wikipedia (Animal Farm 1954 film).

### CLAIM 8: Smith-Mundt Act (1948)

**CONFIRMED**

- The U.S. Information and Educational Exchange Act of 1948, Public Law 80-402, signed by President Truman on January 27, 1948.
- Popularly known as the Smith-Mundt Act (Senator H. Alexander Smith, R-NJ, and Representative Karl E. Mundt, R-SD).
- It authorized the dissemination of U.S. foreign-policy information abroad but prohibited the domestic dissemination of the same material.
- The ban specifically applied to materials produced by the State Department and U.S. government broadcasting entities (Voice of America, Radio Free Europe, etc.).
- Original intent: "Congress, the media and academia would be the filter to bring inside what the State Department said overseas." The law was designed to prevent the U.S. government from doing to its own citizens what defeated Axis powers had done.

**Sources:**
- Public Law 80-402 (1948)
- Cold War Radio Museum
- Northwestern University Law Review analysis
- Wikipedia (Smith-Mundt Act)

### CLAIM 9: Smith-Mundt Modernization Act (2013)

**CONFIRMED (with budget correction needed)**

**Section 1078 of NDAA FY2013:** CONFIRMED. The Smith-Mundt Modernization Act was Section 1078 of H.R. 4310, the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2013.

**Public Law 112-239:** CONFIRMED.

**Signed January 2, 2013 by Obama:** CONFIRMED. The bill passed the House December 20, 2012 (315-107) and the Senate December 21, 2012 (81-14). Signed into law by President Obama on January 2, 2013.

**"Embedded in must-pass defense bill":** CONFIRMED. The NDAA is considered must-pass legislation. The Smith-Mundt provisions were embedded within the larger defense authorization.

**"Struck the domestic dissemination ban":** CONFIRMED. The amendments allowed materials produced by the State Department and the Broadcasting Board of Governors (BBG) to be released within U.S. borders.

**CORRECTION: $810 million budget figure.** The build plan says "annual budget of ~$810 million." This figure is for a LATER year. The BBG's FY2013 budget request was $720 million, not $810 million. The $810 million figure appears to be from approximately FY2020. For accuracy, use $720 million for FY2013, or use "hundreds of millions" without specifying the exact amount.

**"60+ languages":** APPROXIMATELY CONFIRMED. A 2013 U.S. government records document states the combined BBG services broadcast in 59 languages. "60+ languages" is a slight overstatement of the 2013 figure, though later years did exceed 60. The build plan should use "nearly 60 languages" or "59 languages" for precision.

**"First major overhaul in 62 years" vs. "65 years" in opening paragraph:** The build plan's opening paragraph says "For 65 years, it was illegal for the US government to direct propaganda at its own citizens." The Smith-Mundt Act was passed in 1948; the Modernization Act took effect in 2013. That is 65 years. This is correct.

Note: FactCheck.org and USAGM have pushed back on characterizing this as "legalizing propaganda," noting the provisions only apply to the State Department and BBG, not to all government agencies or private media. This nuance could be acknowledged without undermining the scroll's point.

### CLAIM 10: Pentagon Military Analyst Program

**CONFIRMED (with minor number correction)**

**CORRECTION: 75+ analysts should be 74.** The DoD's own records document 74 military analysts participating in the program, not 75+. The Wikipedia article (sourced from DoD records) specifies: "147 events for 74 military analysts." Some secondary sources round up to "75+" but the documented number is 74.

**Program timeline 2002-2008:** CONFIRMED. Launched in early 2002 by then-Assistant Secretary of Defense for Public Affairs Victoria Clarke. The program ended on April 28, 2008, after the NYT expose.

**David Barstow NYT article (April 20, 2008):** CONFIRMED. The article "Behind TV Analysts, Pentagon's Hidden Hand" was published April 20, 2008.

**Pulitzer Prize 2009:** CONFIRMED. The Pulitzer Committee awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Investigative Reporting to The New York Times and David Barstow in April 2009.

**"Message force multipliers" and "surrogates":** CONFIRMED. Internal Pentagon documents explicitly used both terms. Analysts were described as "message force multipliers" and "surrogates" who could be counted on to deliver administration "themes and messages" to millions of Americans "in the form of their own opinions."

**8,000+ FOIA pages:** CONFIRMED. The New York Times obtained over 8,000 pages of emails and documents through a FOIA request backed by lawsuits against the Pentagon.

**No network investigated its own participation:** CONFIRMED. With the exception of two mentions on PBS, there was "a de facto blackout of this story by the networks." CBS, NBC, and Fox provided no comment on their analysts' involvement. No major television network conducted an on-air investigation of its own participation in the program.

**Defense contractor ties:** CONFIRMED. 43 of the 74 analysts had employment or affiliation with defense contractors doing or seeking business with the DoD.

**Program details:** 147 total events: 22 Pentagon meetings, 114 conference calls with senior officials, 11 DoD-sponsored trips to Iraq and Guantanamo Bay.

**GAO report:** CONFIRMED. The GAO released its investigation report in July 2009. The GAO confirmed the program existed and that analysts received special access, but concluded the DoD had not technically violated any laws because "government agencies have traditionally been given wide leeway in how they inform the public about their work."

**FCC response:** CONFIRMED. Representatives Rosa DeLauro and John Dingell filed formal complaints. The FCC investigated whether broadcasters broke "payola" rules but took no enforcement action.

### CLAIM 11: CIA Document 1035-960

**CONFIRMED**

**Declassified via 1976 NYT FOIA:** CONFIRMED. The document was released in response to a 1976 Freedom of Information Act request by the New York Times. **"Conspiracy theory" as weaponized term:** CONFIRMED by the document itself and by Lance deHaven-Smith's analysis.
