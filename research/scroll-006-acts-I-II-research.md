## ACT I -- THE SCIENCE

### 1. Edward Bernays "Propaganda" (1928) -- Opening Quote, Publisher, Family Connection

**CONFIRMED: Opening quote is accurate.** The exact text from the opening of Chapter 1: "The conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is an important element in democratic society. Those who manipulate this unseen mechanism of society constitute an invisible government which is the true ruling power of our country." This matches the plan word-for-word.

**CONFIRMED: Publisher was Horace Liveright.** Published by Horace Liveright Inc., New York, 1928. Multiple first-edition auction records and the Internet Archive confirm this.

**CONFIRMED: Double nephew of Freud.** Bernays was related to Freud through both parents: his mother Anna (1858-1955) was Sigmund Freud's sister, and his father Eli Bernays (1860-1921) was the brother of Freud's wife Martha Bernays. The "double nephew" framing is correct and well-documented.

**Source strength: Excellent.** Primary source (the book itself) is freely available on Internet Archive and Project Gutenberg. Larry Tye's biography "The Father of Spin" (1998) is a standard secondary source.

### 2. Walter Lippmann "Public Opinion" (1922) -- "Manufacture of Consent," New Republic, Wilson Advisor

**CONFIRMED: "Manufacture of consent" phrase appears in "Public Opinion" (1922).** The exact passage: "That the manufacture of consent is capable of great refinements no one, I think, denies." Oxford Reference confirms Lippmann coined the phrase in 1922. Chomsky and Herman later borrowed it for their 1988 book title "Manufacturing Consent."

**CORRECTION: Lippmann was a co-founder of The New Republic, not "co-founder" as a two-person job.** The New Republic was founded in 1914 by three people: Herbert Croly, Walter Lippmann, and Walter Weyl, with financing from Willard and Dorothy Straight. The first issue appeared November 7, 1914. Lippmann left the magazine in 1921 on bad terms with Croly. The plan's claim that Lippmann was "co-founder" is technically correct but should specify it was a three-person founding team if precision matters.

**CONFIRMED: Advisor to President Wilson.** Lippmann helped draft the Fourteen Points. He was recruited to translate the secret treaties of the European Allies into Wilson's peace proposals. He attended the Paris Peace Conference as a U.S. delegate. His role was specifically to "take the secret treaties, analyze the parts which were tolerable, and separate them from those which were regarded as intolerable" (per Colonel House). This is well-documented.

**CONFIRMED: The "specialized class" argument.** Lippmann's core thesis in "Public Opinion" was that the public cannot comprehend complex affairs directly and that a specialized class of experts and media professionals must mediate between events and public perception. This is accurately characterized in the plan.

**Source strength: Excellent.** The full text of "Public Opinion" is available via Project Gutenberg. Lippmann's Wilson advisory role is documented in State Department historical records.

The plan says Lipsky predated "the commonly cited Lippmann" -- this is flatly wrong. Lippmann came first. **Fix: Remove the claim that Lipsky predated Lippmann. The accurate framing is that Lipsky predated Bernays and expanded on themes Lippmann had introduced, but has been largely forgotten.**

The book stands on its own -- it doesn't need Webb's endorsement.**

### 4. Tavistock Institute -- Founding Date, WWII Connection, Sources

**CONFIRMED: Tavistock Institute founded in 1947.** The Tavistock Institute of Human Relations was formally established in September 1947, with a significant grant from the Rockefeller Foundation. This is distinct from the Tavistock Clinic, which was founded in 1920. The plan correctly uses "established in 1947 as an offshoot of the Tavistock Clinic" -- this is accurate.

**CONFIRMED: WWII British Army connection.** Staff from the Tavistock Clinic played key roles in British Army psychiatry during WWII, particularly through the War Office Selection Boards (WOSBs) and Civil Resettlement Units (CRUs). They worked within the Royal Army Medical Corps on innovations in group dynamics and psychological assessment. The post-war institute was created to continue this wartime research in civilian contexts. The plan's characterization of the Institute as growing from "WWII psychological warfare work for the British Army" is essentially accurate, though "psychological warfare" is somewhat stronger language than "army psychiatry and selection" -- the work was more organizational psychology than propaganda per se. **Minor nuance: consider "WWII psychological operations work" rather than "psychological warfare work" if you want precision, though the distinction is arguable.**

**CONFIRMED: Eric Trist & Hugh Murray anthology (1990).** "The Social Engagement of Social Science: A Tavistock Anthology" was edited by Eric Trist, Hugh Murray, and Beulah Trist (Assistant Editor). Volume I (The Socio-Psychological Perspective) was published in 1990 by the University of Pennsylvania Press. It is a three-volume set. This is a real and well-regarded academic work.

**CONFIRMED: Daniel Estulin book.** "Tavistock Institute: Social Engineering the Masses" by Daniel Estulin was published September 22, 2015 by Trine Day. Note: the plan says "(2015)" which is correct. Estulin is a controversial author (also known for "The True Story of the Bilderberg Group"). His Tavistock book is not an academic source -- it is a conspiratorial treatment. **Consider whether to cite it alongside the Trist/Murray anthology, which has genuine academic credibility, or flag Estulin as a secondary/alternative source.**

## ACT II -- THE INFILTRATION (Operation Mockingbird)

### 5. Church Committee (1976) -- Findings, Report Number, Publications Named

**CONFIRMED: Senate Report 94-755 is the correct report number.** The Church Committee's final report was published as Senate Report No. 94-755, "Intelligence Activities and the Rights of Americans" (1976). The committee was formally the "United States Senate Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities," chaired by Senator Frank Church of Idaho.

**CONFIRMED: "Over 50" journalists.** The Church Committee identified over 50 U.S. journalists who had official, secret relationships with the CIA. Church noted this was "probably only the tip of the iceberg" because the CIA refused to provide complete names or organizational affiliations. The plan's "over 50" is correct.

**CORRECTION: The publications list needs careful reframing.** The plan says the Church Committee confirmed CIA infiltration of "Ramparts, Encounter, Partisan Review, and the Paris Review." This is misleading in multiple ways:

- **Ramparts** was NOT infiltrated by the CIA. Ramparts was an independent investigative magazine that EXPOSED CIA covert funding of the National Student Association in April 1967. The CIA actually ran a counter-operation against Ramparts -- CIA operative Edgar Applewhite told author Evan Thomas: "I had all sorts of dirty tricks to hurt their circulation and financing." Ramparts was a target, not an asset. **This is an error that would embarrass us if published.**

- **Encounter** -- CONFIRMED as CIA-funded. The magazine received covert funding from the CIA (and MI6) via the Congress for Cultural Freedom and front organizations like the Farfield Foundation. This was exposed in 1967 by Ramparts, the New York Times, and the Saturday Evening Post. Encounter is the most well-documented CIA literary asset.

- **Partisan Review** -- CONFIRMED as receiving CIA support, but indirectly. The Congress for Cultural Freedom granted Partisan Review $3,000/year for three years in the early 1960s, disguised as foreign magazine subscriptions. It was not a full-fledged CCF magazine but benefited from CIA-adjacent funding.

- **Paris Review** -- CONFIRMED as CIA-connected, but the connection was through co-founder Peter Matthiessen, not through institutional infiltration. Matthiessen was recruited by the CIA (by Yale professor Norman Holmes Pearson) and used The Paris Review as cover while stationed in Paris. Other co-founders George Plimpton and Harold Humes did not know until the 1960s. The Review itself was not directly funded by the CIA.

**CORRECTION: Rewrite the publications list.** Replace "infiltrated major publications including Ramparts, Encounter, Partisan Review, and the Paris Review" with something like: "funded or maintained assets within major publications including Encounter, Partisan Review, and the Paris Review (co-founded by CIA officer Peter Matthiessen). When Ramparts magazine exposed CIA funding of student organizations in 1967, the CIA ran counter-operations to destroy the publication." This is both more accurate and more damning.

**CONFIRMED: Frank Wisner as architect.** Wisner established Operation Mockingbird in 1948 as director of the Office of Policy Coordination (OPC). He referred to his media apparatus as a "Mighty Wurlitzer." He recruited Philip Graham of the Washington Post to run the project within the industry. The plan's characterization of Wisner as Mockingbird's architect is well-documented.

**NOTE on "Operation Mockingbird" naming:** The name "Mockingbird" is somewhat contested in the historical record. Journalist Deborah Davis used the term in "Katharine the Great" (1979), and it has become the standard shorthand. Some historians note that CIA internal documents do not consistently use this specific codename for the media program. The Church Committee itself did not use the term "Operation Mockingbird." The program's existence is thoroughly documented; the specific name is the contested part. **For the scroll, using "Mockingbird" is fine -- it is the universally recognized label -- but avoid claiming the Church Committee used the term.**

### 6. Carl Bernstein Rolling Stone Article (1977)

**CONFIRMED: Published October 20, 1977.** "The CIA and the Media" by Carl Bernstein appeared in Rolling Stone on October 20, 1977. The UNT Finding Aids catalog confirms the exact date. Multiple archives host the full text.

**CONFIRMED: Approximately 25,000 words.** The article is consistently described as a 25,000-word cover story. This is accurate.

**CONFIRMED: "More than 400 journalists."** Bernstein reported that "more than 400 American journalists" secretly carried out assignments for the CIA over the preceding 25 years, "according to documents on file at CIA headquarters." **Minor precision note: The plan uses "over 400" -- the original text says "more than 400." Same meaning, but if quoting directly, use Bernstein's exact phrasing.** The 400 figure is Bernstein's number, derived from CIA documents he reviewed. It is distinct from the Church Committee's "over 50" (which counted only journalists with formal, official relationships). The 400 included a wider range of relationships -- from regular paid agents to those providing occasional assistance.

**CONFIRMED: Named news executives.** Bernstein's article specifically identified relationships with CBS's William Paley, Newsweek's publisher Philip Graham, NYT publisher Arthur Hays Sulzberger, Henry Luce of Time Inc., and James Copley of the Copley News Service.

**Source strength: Excellent.** The article itself is freely available in multiple archives. This is a primary investigative source by one of the most credible American journalists of the 20th century.

### 7. Philip Graham "Call Girl" Quote -- Sourcing and Death

**CORRECTION: The quote attribution is wrong in the plan.** The plan says Graham "told a group of journalists" the call girl quote. According to Deborah Davis in "Katharine the Great," the quote was made BY A CIA OPERATIVE TO Philip Graham during discussions about the availability and pricing of journalists -- not by Graham himself. The CIA agent said to Graham: "You could get a journalist cheaper than a good call girl, for a couple hundred dollars a month." **This is an important attribution error. Graham was the listener, not the speaker.**

**ALTERNATIVE ATTRIBUTION:** Some secondary sources have muddied this further by attributing the quote to Graham directly, or to an unnamed CIA officer speaking generally. The most rigorous attribution is: a CIA operative said this to Graham, as reported by Deborah Davis. **Recommendation: rewrite the evidence card to say something like "A CIA operative reportedly told Philip Graham..." rather than "Graham allegedly told a group of journalists."**

**CONFIRMED: Deborah Davis "Katharine the Great" is real.** First published in 1979 by Harcourt Brace Jovanovich (HBJ). HBJ withdrew the book from circulation weeks after publication, allegedly after a letter from Washington Post executive editor Ben Bradlee. Davis sued HBJ for $6 million for breach of contract; settled for $100,000. The book was later republished. The suppression of the book itself is a relevant media-control data point.

**CONFIRMED: Graham death 1963, ruled suicide.** Philip Leslie Graham (July 18, 1915 -- August 3, 1963) died by suicide with a shotgun at the family's Glen Welby farm in Virginia. He suffered from bipolar disorder. In 1963, at a newspaper publishers' convention, he appeared manic/inebriated and publicly revealed that President Kennedy was having an affair with Mary Pinchot Meyer. He was sedated, placed in a straitjacket, and committed to Chestnut Lodge psychiatric hospital in Rockville, Maryland. He was permitted a weekend visit to the farm, where he died.

**The plan says "died in 1963 under disputed circumstances -- ruled suicide."** The "disputed" framing is a stretch. Graham's mental health crisis was well-documented, and his wife Katharine Graham wrote about it in her memoir "Personal History" (1997). Some conspiracy researchers question the circumstances, but there is no formal dispute or contradictory finding. **Recommendation: say "ruled suicide" without "disputed circumstances" unless you want to specifically cite a source that challenges the ruling. The existing framing implies more controversy than the record supports.**

**Source strength: Excellent.** Saunders' book is the definitive academic treatment of CIA cultural operations. Even the CIA's own review journal engaged with it seriously.

The photo is cataloged at reaganlibrary.gov/archives/photo/c12464-01.

**NOTE: The plan says "Reagan Library photograph" but omits Thomas Bolan.** There were five people in the photo, not four. Bolan was Cohn's law partner and associate. **Either mention all five or acknowledge the fifth person. Omitting Bolan is not an error, but including him strengthens the picture of Cohn's network operation -- he brought his own lawyer to the Oval Office.**

**CONFIRMED: Charles Wick ran USIA.** Wick was director of the United States Information Agency, which oversaw U.S. propaganda operations worldwide. Cohn brokered the relationship between Wick and Murdoch. After the meeting, Murdoch became involved in a privately funded propaganda project to sell Reagan's Central American policies.

**CONFIRMED: CIA Director Casey and Iran-Contra.** William Casey served as CIA Director under Reagan. The Walsh Iran-Contra Report (Chapter 15) documents Casey's role in setting up the covert network to resupply the Contras during the Boland Amendment funding cutoff, and in promoting secret arms sales to Iran (1985-1986). Casey died May 6, 1987 of brain cancer -- just before he was to testify to Congress. He had seizures in December 1986, underwent brain surgery, and never testified. The plan's claim about "illegal domestic propaganda operations promoting Contra support" is supported: the Iran-Contra investigation documented that the Reagan White House (via Oliver North and the Office of Public Diplomacy) ran domestic propaganda campaigns to build public support for the Contras. Whether Casey personally directed the domestic propaganda (vs. the covert operations) is somewhat conflated in the plan. **Recommendation: Be precise -- Casey ran the covert operations; the domestic propaganda was run through the White House Office of Public Diplomacy under Walter Raymond Jr. (a 20-year CIA propaganda veteran transferred to the NSC staff). Both were illegal. Both were connected. But they were distinct operations.**

**CORRECTION: Fox Broadcasting Corporation was founded October 9, 1986, not 1987.** Fox launched with Joan Rivers' "The Late Show" on October 9, 1986. It expanded to primetime on April 5, 1987. The plan says "Fox Broadcasting Corporation founded 1987" -- **this should be corrected to 1986.** The 1987 date likely refers to the primetime expansion, not the founding.

**The plan's implied narrative -- Reagan/Cohn/Murdoch meeting in 1983 leading to Fox in 1986/87 -- is structurally sound.** Cohn brokered the relationship, Murdoch received regulatory favors (including a waiver of foreign ownership rules), and Fox was created. The timeline checks out even with the date correction.
