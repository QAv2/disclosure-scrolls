Act II

The Severance

For the first three centuries after the death of Jesus of Nazareth, there was no single Christian church. There were dozens of communities across the Mediterranean, each with different texts, different practices, different understandings of what the teacher had meant. Some believed in a hierarchy of priests. Others believed every person had direct access to the divine — that the kingdom of heaven was already inside you, and no intermediary was required. The Greek word for this direct knowing was gnosis. The people who practiced it were called Gnostics.

They were about to be erased.

Documented
In 325 AD, Emperor Constantine — unbaptized, fresh from unifying the Roman Empire — convened the Council of Nicaea. Approximately 318 bishops attended. The council produced the Nicene Creed, establishing orthodox doctrine and, by definition, heresy. Only 2 bishops refused to sign — they were exiled. Arius’s writings were ordered burned. Anyone caught hiding them faced the death penalty. Over the following decades, church councils formalized the biblical canon: 27 books included in the New Testament, 50+ known texts excluded. The criterion was not historical accuracy. It was institutional compatibility.
Socrates Scholasticus, Church History I.8. Theodoret, Church History I.7–8. Eusebius, Vita Constantini II.64–72. Bruce Metzger, The Canon of the New Testament (1987).

Documented
In December 1945, an Egyptian farmer named Muhammad Ali al-Samman discovered 13 leather-bound codices buried in a sealed jar near Nag Hammadi, Egypt. The texts had been buried approximately 367 AD — the same year Bishop Athanasius of Alexandria issued his 39th Festal Letter, defining the authorized canon and ordering all non-canonical texts destroyed. Someone buried them instead. The Nag Hammadi library contained 52 texts, including Gnostic gospels that described a radically different Christianity: one where salvation came through direct inner knowledge (gnosis), not through faith in institutional authority (pistis). The texts had been hidden for 1,578 years.
James M. Robinson, ed., The Nag Hammadi Library in English (1978/1990). Elaine Pagels, The Gnostic Gospels (1979, National Book Award).

The Gospel of Thomas — one of the buried texts — records 114 sayings of Jesus with no crucifixion, no resurrection, no priesthood needed. Saying 3: “The kingdom is inside of you, and it is outside of you. When you come to know yourselves, then you will become known.” Saying 70: “If you bring forth what is within you, what you bring forth will save you. If you do not bring forth what is within you, what you do not bring forth will destroy you.”

They didn’t bury these texts because they were wrong. They buried them because they worked. Direct experience doesn’t need a priest. And a church without a congregation is just a building.

Act III

The Burning

The canon was decided. The institution was built. But the direct experience didn’t stop. Across the next twelve centuries, communities kept emerging — mystics, heretics, indigenous traditions, wandering teachers — who claimed you didn’t need permission to access the divine. Every single one was destroyed. Not debated. Not refuted. Destroyed.

And if you track the method of destruction across cultures and centuries, a pattern emerges that has nothing to do with theology and everything to do with access control.

Documented
The Cathars were a Christian movement in southern France who practiced direct spiritual experience, rejected material authority, had no church buildings, and believed every person could access the divine without priestly mediation. In 1209, Pope Innocent III launched the Albigensian Crusade — a 20-year military campaign to exterminate them. At the siege of Béziers (July 22, 1209), Papal legate Arnaud Amalric reportedly said: “Kill them all. God will know his own.” An estimated 20,000 people were massacred at Béziers alone. At Montségur in 1244, over 200 Cathars walked into a bonfire rather than recant. The last known Cathar parfait, Guillaume Belibaste, was burned in 1321.
Mark Gregory Pegg, A Most Holy War (2008). R.I. Moore, The War on Heresy (2012). Malcolm Barber, The Cathars (2000).
Documented
Giordano Bruno was a Dominican friar who proposed that the universe was infinite, that stars were distant suns with their own planets, and that direct experience of the cosmos was communion with the divine. After an 8-year trial by the Roman Inquisition, he was burned alive on February 17, 1600, in the Campo de’ Fiori in Rome. His tongue was nailed to his jaw so he couldn’t speak at the pyre. His works were placed on the Index of Forbidden Books for over 260 years. In the exact square where he was burned, there is now a statue of him — erected in 1889 over Vatican objections.
Ingrid Rowland, Giordano Bruno: Philosopher/Heretic (2008). Frances Yates, Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (1964). Roman Inquisition trial records (Vatican Archives).
